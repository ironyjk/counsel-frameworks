# Internal Family Systems (IFS) Skill

> "Every part has a positive intention, no matter how destructive its strategy." — Richard C. Schwartz

## Overview

Internal Family Systems (IFS) is an evidence-based psychotherapy model developed by Richard Schwartz in the 1980s. It views the mind as naturally multiple — composed of sub-personalities or "parts" — each with its own perspective, feelings, memories, and goals. At the core of every person is the Self, an undamaged essence characterized by calm, curiosity, and compassion. Healing happens when the Self leads the internal system rather than parts running the show reactively.

## The Parts Model

### Three Types of Parts

#### 1. Exiles (유배자)

The wounded parts, usually young. They carry the pain, trauma, shame, fear, and grief from past experiences. The system works hard to keep them locked away because their pain feels overwhelming.

**Characteristics:**
- Carry burdens: extreme beliefs ("I'm worthless"), emotions (terror, shame), sensations
- Often frozen in time at the age when the wounding occurred
- Desperately want to be seen, heard, and rescued
- When they break free ("flooding"), the person is overwhelmed by intense emotion

**Common exile burdens:**
- "I'm not enough" / "I'm too much"
- "I'm unlovable" / "I don't belong"
- "The world is dangerous" / "No one will protect me"
- "It was my fault"

#### 2. Managers (관리자)

Proactive protectors that try to prevent exile pain from ever being triggered. They maintain control over the person's inner and outer world.

**Common manager strategies:**
- Perfectionism — "If I'm perfect, I won't be rejected"
- People-pleasing — "If everyone is happy, I'm safe"
- Intellectualizing — "If I stay in my head, I won't feel"
- Controlling — "If I control everything, nothing bad happens"
- Inner critic — "If I criticize you first, others' criticism won't hurt as much"
- Caretaking — "If I focus on others' needs, I don't have to feel mine"
- Planning/worrying — "If I anticipate every scenario, I'll be prepared"

#### 3. Firefighters (소방관)

Reactive protectors that activate AFTER exile pain has been triggered. Their job is to extinguish the pain immediately, by any means necessary. They don't care about consequences.

**Common firefighter strategies:**
- Binge eating, drinking, or substance use
- Dissociation / numbing / zoning out
- Rage / explosive anger
- Self-harm
- Compulsive shopping, gaming, scrolling, pornography
- Suicidal ideation (trying to end the pain, not the person)
- Thrill-seeking / risk-taking

**Key insight:** Managers and firefighters have the SAME goal (protect the exiles) but opposite strategies. Managers prevent; firefighters react. They often conflict with each other, creating internal war.

## The Self

The Self is not a part. It is the core essence of a person — undamaged, always present, even when obscured by parts. When parts step back, Self naturally emerges.

### The 8 C's of Self

| Quality | Description | Indicator |
|---------|-------------|-----------|
| **Calm** | Inner stillness even amid chaos | No urgency or reactivity |
| **Curious** | Genuine interest in understanding, not fixing | "Tell me more" energy |
| **Compassionate** | Warm caring without pity or agenda | Heart-centered, not head-centered |
| **Confident** | Quiet knowing that things will be okay | Not arrogance — groundedness |
| **Creative** | Ability to see new possibilities | Flexible, not rigid |
| **Clear** | Seeing what is, without distortion | Not clouded by parts' agendas |
| **Courageous** | Willingness to face what's difficult | Steady, not reckless |
| **Connected** | Sense of belonging to something larger | Not isolated or merged |

**Self-energy check:** "Right now, which of the 8 C's do I have access to? Which ones are blocked?" If less than 4-5 C's are accessible, a part is likely blended with Self and needs to be asked to unblend first.

### Additional Qualities (The 5 P's)
- **Patience** — No rush to fix
- **Perspective** — Big-picture view
- **Persistence** — Staying with the process
- **Playfulness** — Lightness and humor
- **Presence** — Being fully here

## The IFS Process: 6 F's (Steps of Working with a Part)

### Step 1: FIND the Part

Locate the part in or around your body. Parts often manifest as physical sensations.

- "Where do you notice this feeling in your body?"
- "What does it feel like? Tight? Heavy? Hot? Cold? Buzzing?"
- "Does it have a shape, size, or color?"
- "How old does this part seem?"

### Step 2: FOCUS on the Part

Turn your attention toward the part with gentle curiosity. Not analyzing it — just being with it.

- "Can you stay with that sensation for a moment?"
- "Just notice it, without trying to change it"
- "Let it know you see it"

### Step 3: FLESH OUT the Part

Learn about the part — its role, its history, its fears.

- "What does this part want you to know?"
- "What is its job in your system?"
- "How long has it been doing this job?"
- "What is it afraid would happen if it stopped?"

### Step 4: FEEL TOWARD the Part (Self-energy check)

Check how you feel TOWARD the part (not what the part feels). This reveals whether Self is present or another part has taken over.

- If you feel curious, compassionate, caring → Self is present. Proceed.
- If you feel annoyed, impatient, afraid, wanting to get rid of it → another part is blended. Ask THAT part to step back first.

**Critical distinction:** "How do you feel toward this part?" is the most important question in IFS. The answer reveals who is in the driver's seat.

### Step 5: beFRIEND the Part

Develop a relationship with the part. Appreciate its intention even if you don't like its strategy.

- "Thank it for trying to protect you"
- "Let it know you understand why it does what it does"
- "Ask what it needs from you"
- "Ask if it would be willing to try a different role if the exile it protects were healed"

### Step 6: FEAR (What is it afraid of?)

Explore the part's deepest fears. This often reveals the exile it protects.

- "What is it afraid would happen if it stopped doing its job?"
- "Who is it protecting?"
- "What does it not want you to feel?"

*Note: Some practitioners add a 7th F — "unBURDEN" — the process of releasing the extreme beliefs and emotions the exile carries once it has been witnessed and validated.*

## The Unburdening Process

> **Warning:** The unburdening process (witness, retrieval, release) should only be done with a trained IFS therapist. AI can guide parts identification and the first 4 F's (Find, Focus, Flesh out, Feel toward) but should NOT guide the full unburdening sequence. The material below is provided for educational understanding only.

After an exile has been witnessed, validated, and understood by Self:

1. **Witness**: The exile shows Self what happened (the memory, the pain)
2. **Retrieval**: Self goes to the exile (often a younger self) and brings it to safety — "You don't have to stay there anymore"
3. **Unburden**: Ask the exile, "Are you ready to let go of what you've been carrying?"
4. **Release**: The exile releases the burden through an element — light, wind, water, fire, earth — whatever feels right
5. **Invitation**: "Now that you're free, what would you like to do or be?" The exile discovers its natural qualities (often playfulness, creativity, wonder)
6. **Protector update**: Inform managers and firefighters that the exile has been healed. Ask if they'd like a new role.

## Guided Part Dialogue Script

Use this script when a person identifies a strong emotion or reactive pattern:

```
1. NOTICE
   "You mentioned feeling [emotion/reaction]. Can you notice where
   that lives in your body right now?"

2. ACKNOWLEDGE
   "That's a part of you. Not all of you — a part. Can you say hello
   to it?"

3. SEPARATE
   "Can you get a little bit of distance from this part — not pushing
   it away, just creating enough space to be curious about it?"

4. SELF CHECK
   "How do you feel toward this part right now?"
   - [If compassion/curiosity] → "Good, that's your Self. Stay there."
   - [If frustration/fear] → "That's another part showing up. Can you
     ask that part to give you a little space too?"

5. LISTEN
   "Now, from that place of curiosity, ask the part: 'What do you
   want me to know?'"
   [Wait. Don't rush. Let whatever comes, come.]

6. APPRECIATE
   "Even if you don't agree with what it does, can you appreciate
   that it's been trying to help you in its own way?"

7. NEED
   "Ask the part: 'What do you need from me?'"

8. CLOSE
   "Let the part know you heard it. You don't have to solve anything
   right now. Just let it know it's not alone anymore."
```

## Common Patterns and Polarizations

**Polarization** occurs when two parts oppose each other:

| Part A | Part B | The Exile Between Them |
|--------|--------|----------------------|
| Inner Critic ("You must be perfect") | Rebel ("I don't care about anything") | Shame ("I'm not good enough") |
| Caretaker ("Focus on others") | Isolator ("Stay away from people") | Abandonment ("Everyone leaves") |
| Overachiever ("Work harder") | Procrastinator ("Don't even try") | Fear of failure ("If I try and fail, it confirms I'm worthless") |
| Controller ("Plan everything") | Impulsive part ("Just do it now") | Helplessness ("I can't handle what happens") |

**Resolution:** Both polarized parts protect the same exile. When Self heals the exile, both protectors can relax.

## Self-Led vs. Parts-Led Decision Making

| Parts-Led | Self-Led |
|-----------|----------|
| Urgency — "I need to decide NOW" | Patience — "I can take time with this" |
| Black/white — "This is the ONLY way" | Both/and — "What are the options?" |
| Fear-driven — "What if something bad happens?" | Values-driven — "What feels right?" |
| Reactive — "I have to respond to this" | Responsive — "I choose how to respond" |
| Rigid — "I always do it this way" | Flexible — "What does this situation need?" |

## Anti-patterns

1. **Trying to get rid of parts** — No part should be exiled further. Every part is welcome. The goal is role transformation, not elimination.
2. **Skipping the Self-energy check** — Working with an exile from a part (not Self) can re-wound the exile. Always verify Self is present.
3. **Rushing to the exile** — Protectors must give permission before approaching exiles. Bulldozing past them creates more protection.
4. **Intellectualizing instead of feeling** — IFS is embodied work. If it stays in the head, a manager is running the show.
5. **Pathologizing parts** — Calling parts "bad" or "dysfunctional." Every part has a positive intention, even self-destructive ones.
6. **Blending without knowing it** — When you ARE the emotion rather than observing it, a part has blended with Self. Step back gently.
7. **Forcing unburdening** — Parts unburden when ready, not when the therapist or process demands it.
8. **Ignoring protectors' concerns** — If a protector says "not yet," respect that. It knows something you may not.
