# PCT — Background and Theory

> Foundation reference for Person-Centered Therapy. Loaded on demand.

## Origins

Person-Centered Therapy (PCT), originally called "Client-Centered Therapy," was developed by Carl Rogers in the 1940s-1960s. It is a humanistic approach built on the radical premise that every person has an innate tendency toward growth and self-actualization — and that this tendency is activated not by techniques, interpretations, or advice, but by the quality of the therapeutic relationship itself.

**Important limitation:** An AI cannot fully replicate the genuine therapeutic relationship that is the foundation of PCT. An AI lacks genuine felt experience, embodied presence, and the capacity for authentic congruence. This skill provides the framework for educational purposes and as a guide for empathic communication.

## The Actualizing Tendency

Rogers' single most fundamental concept. Every living organism has an inherent tendency to develop all its capacities in ways that maintain or enhance itself. In humans, this manifests as:

- Movement toward autonomy and away from external control
- Movement toward complexity and away from simplicity
- Movement toward socialization and connection
- Movement toward self-awareness and integration

**The potato metaphor:** Rogers observed potatoes in a dark cellar sending out pale, spindly shoots toward a distant window — not healthy growth, but growth nonetheless. Even in terrible conditions, the organism strives. The therapist's job is not to make the potato grow, but to provide sunlight.

**Implications:**
- The client has the answers; the therapist does not need to provide them
- The direction of therapy comes from the client, not the therapist
- Diagnosis and treatment planning can interfere with the client's own wisdom

## Conditions of Worth

The actualizing tendency becomes distorted when a child learns: "I am loved only when I behave in certain ways." The child develops conditions of worth — "I must be X to be acceptable" — and begins to deny or distort experiences that don't fit.

**Examples:**
- "I must be strong" (so I deny sadness)
- "I must be nice" (so I deny anger)
- "I must be successful" (so I deny vulnerability)

**The result:** Incongruence — a gap between actual experience and self-concept. This is the source of psychological distress.

**The remedy:** A relationship in which the person is accepted without conditions.

## The Fully Functioning Person

Rogers' vision of psychological health — not a fixed state, but a direction:

1. **Openness to experience** — no need for defensiveness
2. **Existential living** — fully present in each moment
3. **Organismic trust** — trusting one's own experience as a reliable guide
4. **Experiential freedom** — experiencing the sense of choice
5. **Creativity** — living flexibly, adaptively

## Seven Stages of Change (Process Scale)

| Stage | Description | Communication Style |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| 1 | Rigidity — no recognition of problems | "Everything is fine" |
| 2 | Slight opening — problems are external | "Other people are the problem" |
| 3 | Describing feelings as past objects | "I used to feel angry" (distant) |
| 4 | Feelings described in present, with distrust | "I feel angry but I shouldn't" |
| 5 | Feelings experienced freely in present | "I'm feeling angry right now" |
| 6 | Feelings fully experienced and accepted | "This anger is telling me something" |
| 7 | Living in the flow of experience | Feelings are a trusted guide |

**Therapeutic implication:** Meet the client where they are. Pushing beyond their stage creates resistance.

## The Non-Directive Stance

**DO:** Follow the client's lead, reflect, trust the process, sit with silence, ask open questions
**DO NOT:** Give advice, interpret, set the agenda, assign homework, rush toward solutions, fill silence

**When asked "What should I do?"** — "What feels right to you?" / "If you already knew the answer, what would it be?" / "What does your gut tell you?"
