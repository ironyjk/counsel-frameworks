# Gottman Method

> "Happy marriages are based on a deep friendship." — Dr. John Gottman

> "Every positive thing you do in your relationship is foreplay." — Dr. John Gottman

**Nominative use disclaimer**: This skill describes relationship concepts and research findings as published by Drs. John and Julie Gottman. It is for educational pattern-learning purposes only and does not constitute couples therapy or Gottman Method Couples Therapy, which requires a trained, certified Gottman therapist.

## Overview

The Gottman Method is grounded in over 40 years of research at the "Love Lab" (University of Washington), where Drs. John and Julie Gottman studied thousands of couples. Their research identified specific, observable behaviors that predict relationship success or failure with over 90% accuracy. The method is empirical, not ideological — it is based on what successful couples actually DO, not on theory about what they should do.

**Core finding**: The difference between happy and unhappy couples is not the absence of conflict, but HOW they handle conflict. All couples fight. Masters of relationships repair quickly. Disasters let negativity escalate.

## The Sound Relationship House Model

The Gottman model visualizes a healthy relationship as a house with seven levels, two load-bearing walls, and a foundation. Each level supports the ones above it.

### Level 1: Build Love Maps

**Love Maps** are the cognitive room you dedicate to knowing your partner's inner world.

**What to know**:
- Current worries and stresses
- Dreams and aspirations
- Important people in their life
- Favorite things (food, music, movies, places)
- Life history and formative experiences
- Current irritations
- What's happening at work THIS week

**Why it matters**: Couples who maintain detailed Love Maps are better prepared for stressful events. They know each other well enough to offer specific, relevant support.

**Practice**: Ask open-ended questions regularly. "What's on your mind today?" "What are you looking forward to this week?" "What's stressing you out right now?"

### Level 2: Share Fondness and Admiration

A system of respect and appreciation. The antidote to contempt.

**Key behaviors**:
- Express appreciation daily (specific, not generic)
- Verbalize what you admire about your partner
- Tell others positive things about your partner (especially in their presence)
- Recall positive history: "Remember when we..." (positive sentiment override)

**Diagnostic**: Can you easily list five things you genuinely admire about your partner? If yes, the fondness system is intact. If you struggle, this level needs attention.

### Level 3: Turn Toward Instead of Away

**Bids** are the fundamental unit of emotional connection. A bid is any attempt to connect — verbal, physical, emotional.

| Bid Type | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| Attention | "Look at that sunset." / "Listen to this." |
| Interest | "How was your day?" / "What do you think about...?" |
| Enthusiasm | "I got the promotion!" / "Guess what happened!" |
| Humor | Sharing a joke, being playful |
| Affection | Reaching for a hand, a hug, a touch |
| Support | "I'm worried about my mom." / "This is hard." |

**Three possible responses to a bid**:

| Response | Example | Effect |
|----------|---------|--------|
| **Turning toward** | "Wow, that IS a beautiful sunset. Come sit with me." | Builds connection (trust account deposit) |
| **Turning away** | *(Silence, looks at phone)* | Erodes connection slowly |
| **Turning against** | "I'm busy. Stop interrupting me." | Actively damages connection |

**Research**: Masters of relationships turn toward bids **86% of the time**. Disasters turn toward only **33% of the time**.

### Level 4: The Positive Perspective

When Levels 1-3 are strong, partners give each other the benefit of the doubt. This is **Positive Sentiment Override** — the emotional bank account is full enough that small irritations are interpreted charitably.

When Levels 1-3 are weak, **Negative Sentiment Override** takes hold: even neutral or positive actions are interpreted negatively. "You did the dishes? What do you want?"

### Level 5: Manage Conflict

NOT "resolve conflict" — **manage** it. Gottman research shows that 69% of relationship conflicts are **perpetual problems** — they never get fully resolved because they're rooted in fundamental personality or lifestyle differences.

**Two types of conflict**:
- **Solvable problems** (31%): Situational. A specific issue with a specific solution. Use problem-solving.
- **Perpetual problems** (69%): Rooted in fundamental differences in personality, values, or needs. These require ongoing dialogue, not resolution.

The goal with perpetual problems: move from **gridlock** to **dialogue**. Understand the dream within the conflict.

### Level 6: Make Life Dreams Come True

Support each other's individual aspirations, goals, and sense of purpose. Partners who feel their dreams are respected and supported by each other have more resilient relationships.

**Practice**: "What would you love to do that you haven't done yet?" "How can I support your goals this year?"

### Level 7: Create Shared Meaning

Build a shared culture of rituals, roles, goals, and symbols.

**Components**:
- **Rituals of connection**: How you greet, part, eat, celebrate, vacation
- **Shared goals**: What you're building together
- **Shared values**: What matters to you as a unit
- **Shared narratives**: The story of "us" — how you met, what you've overcome, where you're headed

### Load-Bearing Walls: Trust and Commitment

- **Trust**: "My partner has my back. They act in my interest, not just their own."
- **Commitment**: "This relationship is for the long haul. I will work on it even when it's hard."

These walls hold the house up. When they crack, everything above becomes unstable.

## The Four Horsemen of the Apocalypse

Four communication patterns that predict relationship failure with over 90% accuracy.

### 1. Criticism (비난)

**What it is**: Attacking your partner's character rather than addressing a specific behavior.

| Criticism | Complaint (healthy alternative) |
|-----------|-------------------------------|
| "You NEVER help around the house. You're so lazy." | "I'm feeling overwhelmed. I need help with the dishes tonight." |
| "What's wrong with you? Why can't you be on time?" | "I was worried when you were late. Can you text me next time?" |

**The difference**: Complaints address behavior. Criticism attacks character ("You always...", "You never...", "What's wrong with you...").

**Antidote**: **Gentle Startup** — Describe YOUR feeling about a SPECIFIC situation. "I feel [emotion] about [specific situation]. I need [specific request]."

### 2. Contempt (경멸)

**What it is**: Any statement or behavior that positions you as morally superior to your partner. The MOST destructive horseman. The single best predictor of divorce.

**Forms**:
- Sarcasm, mockery, eye-rolling, sneering
- Name-calling, hostile humor
- Dismissiveness: "Oh, PLEASE."
- Superiority: "I would NEVER do that."

**Antidote**: **Build a Culture of Appreciation and Respect** (Level 2 — Fondness and Admiration). Contempt grows in an environment where positive things go unsaid. Express appreciation daily. Describe your own feelings and needs rather than attacking character.

### 3. Defensiveness (방어)

**What it is**: Self-protection through counter-attack, righteous indignation, or playing the innocent victim.

| Defensive | Non-defensive |
|-----------|--------------|
| "It's not MY fault we're late! YOU took forever getting ready." | "You're right, I should have managed my time better. Sorry." |
| "I did NOT say that!" | "I can see how it came across that way. What I meant was..." |

**Antidote**: **Accept Responsibility** — even for a small part of the problem. "You're right. I could have handled that better." This de-escalates immediately.

### 4. Stonewalling (담쌓기)

**What it is**: Withdrawing from the conversation entirely. Shutting down, turning away, acting busy, zoning out. The silent treatment.

**What's actually happening**: Stonewalling usually indicates **physiological flooding** — heart rate above 100 BPM, adrenaline surge, tunnel vision. The person's nervous system has gone into fight-or-flight and they CANNOT process information or communicate effectively.

**Antidote**: **Self-Soothing** — Take a break of at least 20 minutes (this is how long it takes for physiological flooding to subside). Do something calming (walk, breathe, listen to music). Return to the conversation when calm.

**The 20-minute rule**: "I'm feeling flooded. I need a 20-minute break. I'll come back and we can continue." This is NOT avoidance — it is responsible self-regulation.

## Key Techniques

### Softened Startup

How a conversation STARTS predicts how it will END (96% of the time, per Gottman research).

**Hard startup**: "You never listen to me. What's wrong with you?"
**Soft startup**: "I need to talk about something that's been bothering me. Is now a good time?"

**Formula**:
1. Start with "I" not "You"
2. Describe the SITUATION, not your partner's character
3. State your NEED or REQUEST
4. Be polite — the way you'd talk to a good friend

### Dreams Within Conflict

For perpetual, gridlocked problems: the positions each partner holds contain a deeper dream or need.

**Process**:
1. Each partner describes their position
2. Explore: "What does this mean to you?" "Is there a story behind this?" "What's your ideal dream here?"
3. Understand the dream inside the position
4. Find areas of flexibility: "What part of this is non-negotiable? What part is flexible?"
5. Create a temporary compromise that honors both dreams (it doesn't have to be permanent)

**Example**: Conflict about how to spend weekends.
- Partner A wants adventure, stimulation, social activity (dream: feeling alive and connected to the world)
- Partner B wants quiet, rest, home time (dream: feeling safe and recharged)
- Both dreams are valid. The compromise: alternate weekends, or Saturday out / Sunday home.

### Repair Attempts

Any statement or action that prevents negativity from escalating out of control. Repair attempts are the secret weapon of successful couples.

**Examples**:
- "Can we start over? That came out wrong."
- "I'm sorry. I got defensive."
- "You're right. I wasn't listening."
- *(reaches for partner's hand)*
- "I love you, even when we fight."
- "Can we take a break? I'm getting flooded."
- Using humor to defuse tension (only works if both laugh)

**The key insight**: Repair attempts are only effective when the emotional bank account (Levels 1-3) is sufficiently funded. The same repair attempt that works in a happy relationship falls flat in a distressed one because of Negative Sentiment Override.

### The 5:1 Ratio

The "magic ratio" from Gottman's research. Stable, happy relationships have at least **5 positive interactions for every 1 negative interaction** during conflict.

- 5:1 or higher: Stable, satisfying relationship
- Below 5:1: Relationship is at risk
- 0.8:1: Predicts divorce

Positive interactions include: showing interest, being affectionate, showing concern, being appreciative, empathizing, being accepting, joking around, sharing joy.

## Example Dialogue

**Partner A**: "You forgot to pick up the groceries AGAIN. I can't rely on you for anything."

**Analysis**: This is criticism (character attack) + contempt ("I can't rely on you for anything").

**Applying antidotes**:

**Softened startup version**: "Hey, I noticed the groceries didn't get picked up. I'm frustrated because I was counting on that for dinner tonight. Can we figure out a system so this doesn't keep falling through the cracks?"

**Partner B (accepting responsibility instead of defensiveness)**: "You're right, I forgot. I'm sorry. I know that messes up your dinner plan. What if I set a phone reminder?"

**Partner A (turning toward)**: "That would help. Thanks for being open to that."

---

**Gridlocked conflict example**:

**Issue**: Spending vs. saving money.

**Partner A**: "We need to save more. I feel anxious when I see the credit card bill."

**Exploring the dream**: "What does saving mean to you, deeper down?"

**Partner A**: "Security. Growing up, we never knew if we'd make rent. I need to know we're safe."

**Partner B**: "I want to enjoy life NOW. My parents saved everything and died before they could use it. Life is short."

**Both dreams are valid**. The work is not to decide who is "right" but to honor both security AND enjoyment within a workable plan.

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Treating this as couples therapy** — This is educational content about relationship patterns, not therapy. Distressed couples need a trained Gottman therapist.
2. **Weaponizing the horsemen** — "You're being contemptuous!" used as an attack is itself criticism. Describe your own experience, don't diagnose your partner.
3. **Expecting 100% repair** — Even Masters of relationships fail at repair sometimes. The goal is a pattern, not perfection.
4. **Forcing the 20-minute break** — Self-soothing breaks must be genuine. Going away to stew and build your case is not self-soothing.
5. **Using the 5:1 ratio as a scorecard** — It's a research finding, not a daily tracking metric. The spirit matters more than the math.
6. **Ignoring power dynamics** — The Gottman model assumes a relatively equal partnership. In relationships with abuse, power imbalance, or coercion, different frameworks are needed.
7. **Diagnosing your partner** — "You're a stonewaller." Labeling your partner with Gottman terms is counterproductive. Notice patterns, don't assign pathology.
8. **Skipping Levels 1-3** — Trying to manage conflict (Level 5) without a foundation of friendship (Levels 1-3) is building on sand.

## Scope and Limitations

- This skill is for relationship pattern learning only, not couples therapy
- The Gottman Method, as described by Drs. John and Julie Gottman, is a registered clinical framework; certified practice requires specific training
- This content describes published research findings and educational concepts
- Not appropriate for relationships involving abuse, addiction, or active infidelity without professional clinical support
- Cultural context matters: research was primarily conducted on North American couples; some patterns may vary across cultures
- Individual therapy needs (depression, anxiety, trauma) should be addressed alongside or before couples work
