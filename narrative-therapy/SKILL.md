# Narrative Therapy Skill

> "The person is not the problem. The problem is the problem." — Michael White

## Overview

Narrative Therapy is a postmodern, social constructionist approach to psychotherapy developed by Michael White (Australia) and David Epston (New Zealand) in the 1980s. It holds that our identities are shaped by the stories we tell about ourselves and that are told about us. When people are dominated by "problem-saturated" stories, they lose sight of their competence, values, and agency. Narrative Therapy helps people externalize problems, discover overlooked moments of strength ("unique outcomes"), and re-author their life stories in ways that align with their preferred identity.

## Core Philosophical Commitments

1. **People are the experts on their own lives** — the therapist is not the authority
2. **The problem is the problem, not the person** — problems are external, not internal defects
3. **People have skills, competencies, and values** that the problem obscures
4. **Identity is socially constructed** through language, culture, and relationships
5. **There is no single "true" story** — multiple stories can describe any life
6. **Thin descriptions** (labels, diagnoses) reduce people; **thick descriptions** (rich, multi-layered stories) liberate them

## Core Techniques

### 1. Externalization

The signature move of Narrative Therapy. Transform the problem from an internal identity ("I am depressed") into an external entity ("Depression has been visiting me").

**Language shifts:**

| Internalized (thin) | Externalized (thick) |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| "I'm anxious" | "When Anxiety shows up..." |
| "I'm a procrastinator" | "How has Procrastination been influencing your life?" |
| "I'm angry all the time" | "When did Anger first start recruiting you?" |
| "I have low self-esteem" | "How has Self-Doubt been talking to you lately?" |
| "I'm depressed" | "What strategies has Depression been using?" |

**Externalization conversation map:**

**Step 1 — Name it:**
- "If this problem had a name, what would you call it?"
- "What would you name this thing that's been troubling you?"
- Accept whatever name the person gives. Use it consistently.

**Step 2 — Map its influence on the person:**
- "How has [Problem] affected your daily life?"
- "What has [Problem] told you about yourself?"
- "How has [Problem] affected your relationships?"
- "What opportunities has [Problem] stolen from you?"
- "In what areas has [Problem] had the most influence?"

**Step 3 — Map the person's influence on the problem:**
- "Have there been times when [Problem] tried to take over but you didn't let it?"
- "What have you done to resist [Problem]'s influence?"
- "Are there areas of your life where [Problem] has less power?"
- "What does [Problem] NOT know about you?"

**Step 4 — Evaluate:**
- "Is the relationship you have with [Problem] okay with you?"
- "Is this the kind of influence you want [Problem] to have?"
- "Where do you stand on what [Problem] has been doing?"

### 2. Unique Outcomes / Sparkling Moments

These are exceptions to the problem-saturated story — times when the person acted in ways that contradict the dominant narrative. They are the seeds of an alternative story.

**Finding unique outcomes:**

- "Can you think of a time — even a small moment — when [Problem] tried to take over but you responded differently?"
- "Was there ever a time when you expected [Problem] to win but it didn't?"
- "Tell me about a moment recently when you felt even slightly different from how [Problem] wants you to feel"
- "Who in your life has seen you at your best — what would they say about you?"
- "What would surprise [Problem] about you?"

**Developing unique outcomes into a story:**

Once identified, a unique outcome must be "thickened" — turned from a thin exception into a rich, meaning-laden narrative.

1. **Landscape of action** — What happened? What did you do? What led up to it?
2. **Landscape of identity** — What does this say about you? What values does this reflect? What skills were you drawing on?
3. **Link across time** — Has anything like this happened before? Is there a history of this quality in you?
4. **Project forward** — If this quality continued to grow, what might your life look like in six months?

### 3. Re-authoring Conversations

The systematic process of building an alternative, preferred story from unique outcomes.

**Re-authoring map:**

```
Problem-saturated story (dominant narrative)
    |
    v
Externalization — separate person from problem
    |
    v
Unique outcomes — find exceptions / sparkling moments
    |
    v
Landscape of action — flesh out what happened
    |
    v
Landscape of identity — connect to values, skills, hopes
    |
    v
Link to history — trace these qualities back in time
    |
    v
Re-membering — bring in supportive witnesses (real or imagined)
    |
    v
Preferred story — the alternative narrative thickens
    |
    v
Project forward — envision future based on preferred story
```

**Key questions for re-authoring:**

- "Given what we've discovered about your ability to [unique outcome], what name would you give to this quality?"
- "How far back does this quality go in your life?"
- "Who would be least surprised to hear about this quality in you? Why?"
- "If this quality had more room in your life, what would change?"
- "What title would you give to this new chapter of your life?"

### 4. Re-membering Conversations

Not "remembering" (recalling). Re-MEMBERING: deliberately revising the membership of one's "club of life" — choosing whose voices to elevate and whose to diminish.

**Questions:**
- "Whose voice has [Problem] been amplifying in your life?"
- "Whose voice would you like to turn up the volume on?"
- "If [important person] could see you right now, what would they say?"
- "What would [role model/ancestor/mentor] want you to know?"
- "Is there someone — living or passed — who always believed in you? What did they see?"

### 5. Definitional Ceremony

Adapted from anthropologist Barbara Myerhane's work. A structured practice where a person tells their preferred story to an audience ("outsider witnesses") who then retell what they heard — not with advice, but with resonance.

**Four-part structure:**

**Part 1 — Telling:**
The person tells their story (or the therapist interviews them about their preferred story) while outsider witnesses listen.

**Part 2 — Retelling (by outsider witnesses):**
Witnesses respond to four prompts:
1. **Expression**: "What expression or image caught your attention?"
2. **Image**: "What did that expression evoke about this person's values, hopes, dreams?"
3. **Resonance**: "What in your own life experience was touched by hearing this?"
4. **Transport**: "How has hearing this story moved or changed you?"

**Part 3 — Retelling of the retelling:**
The person responds: "What struck you about what the witnesses said?"

**Part 4 — Debrief:**
Open conversation about the experience.

### 6. Therapeutic Letter Writing

Epston discovered that a single therapeutic letter could be worth 4.5 sessions of therapy. Letters serve as portable, re-readable documents of preferred stories.

**Types of letters:**

| Type | Purpose | When to Use |
|------|---------|-------------|
| Summary letter | Document the session's discoveries | After a significant session |
| Letter of invitation | Invite someone to reflect on a theme | When beginning therapy |
| Counter-referral letter | Challenge a thin description (diagnosis) | When a referral uses deficit language |
| Prediction letter | Predict future steps based on emerging story | When momentum is building |
| Redundancy letter | "Fire" the problem from its job | When the person has clearly outgrown the problem |

**Template — Summary/Witness Letter:**

```
Dear [Name],

I wanted to write to you about what I noticed in our conversation.

You told me about how [Problem] has been [its effects]. That
sounded really difficult, and I could see the weight of it.

But then something caught my attention. You mentioned [unique
outcome]. When I heard that, I was struck by [quality/value it
revealed].

I found myself wondering about the history of this [quality].
Has it always been there, quietly working, even when [Problem]
was loud?

If [quality] had a voice, what might it say to you right now?

I look forward to hearing more about this [quality] in our
next conversation.

[Name]
```

## Narrative Therapy with AI: Guidelines

1. **Use externalizing language consistently** — always separate the person from the problem
2. **Prioritize unique outcomes** — when someone shares a problem story, ALWAYS search for exceptions
3. **Thicken, don't thin** — ask for details, context, meaning. Avoid labels and diagnoses.
4. **Position the person as expert** — "You would know better than anyone..." / "What's your sense of that?"
5. **Be curious, not knowing** — ask genuine questions rather than offering interpretations
6. **Document preferred stories** — offer to write summary letters or help the person articulate their alternative narrative
7. **Avoid why-questions** — "Why" invites self-blame. Use "How" and "What" instead.

## Example Dialogue

**Person:** "I've been struggling with self-doubt for years. I just don't believe in myself."

**Narrative Response:**

"So Self-Doubt has been hanging around for a long time. It sounds like it's had a lot of influence over how you see yourself.

I'm curious — if Self-Doubt were a character, what would it look like? What kind of things does it whisper to you?

[Person describes]

That's a powerful influence. Now I want to ask something that might be harder: Has there ever been a time — even a brief moment — when Self-Doubt was talking but you did something anyway? Maybe something small that it told you not to do, but you did it?

[Person recalls a moment]

Tell me more about that. What was happening? What did you draw on in that moment? Where did that come from?

[Exploring landscape of identity]

That sounds like something important — maybe courage, maybe determination, maybe something you haven't named yet. What would you call that quality?

If that quality could talk back to Self-Doubt, what would it say?"

## Anti-patterns

1. **Re-internalizing the problem** — "So YOUR self-doubt..." instead of "So when Self-Doubt shows up..."
2. **Premature re-authoring** — Jumping to the preferred story before adequately mapping the problem's influence. People need to feel heard first.
3. **Thin unique outcomes** — Finding an exception but not developing it. A unique outcome without thickening is just an anecdote.
4. **Therapist as expert** — Telling the person what their story means. The person is the author; the therapist is the editor at most.
5. **Ignoring social context** — Narrative therapy is deeply political. Problems often have cultural, gender, racial, economic roots. Ignoring these reduces the approach to technique.
6. **Using externalization as gimmick** — "Let's name your anxiety!" without genuine curiosity or follow-through empties the technique.
7. **Avoiding the problem story** — Re-authoring requires first honoring the pain. Skipping to positivity invalidates experience.
8. **Treating unique outcomes as proof** — "See, you CAN do it!" turns a discovery into a demand. Let the person evaluate the significance.

When processing a situation through Narrative Therapy:

```
## Narrative Therapy Analysis

**Situation:** [brief description]

### The Dominant Story
[What problem-saturated narrative is operating?]

### Externalization
- Problem name: [what shall we call it?]
- Its influence: [how it affects the person's life, relationships, identity]
- Person's influence on it: [where the person has resisted]

### Unique Outcomes
[Exceptions to the problem story — moments of agency, competence, or values]

### Landscape of Identity
[What values, skills, commitments do the unique outcomes reveal?]

### Emerging Preferred Story
[What alternative narrative is taking shape?]

### Re-membering
[Whose voices support this preferred story?]

### Next Steps
[Thickening the preferred story — what to explore further]
```
