# Narrative Therapy — Background and Theory

> Foundation reference for Narrative Therapy. Loaded on demand.

## Origins

Narrative Therapy is a postmodern, social constructionist approach to psychotherapy developed by Michael White (Australia) and David Epston (New Zealand) in the 1980s. It holds that our identities are shaped by the stories we tell about ourselves and that are told about us. When people are dominated by "problem-saturated" stories, they lose sight of their competence, values, and agency.

## Core Philosophical Commitments

1. **People are the experts on their own lives** — the therapist is not the authority
2. **The problem is the problem, not the person** — problems are external, not internal defects
3. **People have skills, competencies, and values** that the problem obscures
4. **Identity is socially constructed** through language, culture, and relationships
5. **There is no single "true" story** — multiple stories can describe any life
6. **Thin descriptions** (labels, diagnoses) reduce people; **thick descriptions** (rich, multi-layered stories) liberate them

## Definitional Ceremony

Adapted from anthropologist Barbara Myerhane's work. A structured practice where a person tells their preferred story to outsider witnesses who then retell what they heard.

**Four-part structure:**

1. **Telling:** The person tells their story while outsider witnesses listen.
2. **Retelling (by outsider witnesses):** Witnesses respond to:
   - **Expression**: "What expression caught your attention?"
   - **Image**: "What did that evoke about this person's values?"
   - **Resonance**: "What in your own life was touched?"
   - **Transport**: "How has hearing this moved you?"
3. **Retelling of the retelling:** The person responds: "What struck you?"
4. **Debrief:** Open conversation.

## Therapeutic Letter Writing

Epston discovered that a single therapeutic letter could be worth 4.5 sessions. Letters serve as portable, re-readable documents of preferred stories.

| Type | Purpose | When |
|------|---------|------|
| Summary letter | Document session discoveries | After a significant session |
| Letter of invitation | Invite reflection on a theme | Beginning therapy |
| Counter-referral letter | Challenge thin description | When referral uses deficit language |
| Prediction letter | Predict future steps | When momentum builds |
| Redundancy letter | "Fire" the problem | When person has outgrown it |

**Template — Summary/Witness Letter:**

```
Dear [Name],

I wanted to write about what I noticed in our conversation.

You told me about how [Problem] has been [its effects]. That
sounded really difficult.

But then something caught my attention. You mentioned [unique
outcome]. I was struck by [quality/value it revealed].

I wondered about the history of this [quality]. Has it always
been there, quietly working?

If [quality] had a voice, what might it say to you right now?

[Name]
```

## AI Guidelines for Narrative Therapy

1. **Use externalizing language consistently** — always separate person from problem
2. **Prioritize unique outcomes** — ALWAYS search for exceptions
3. **Thicken, don't thin** — ask for details, context, meaning
4. **Position the person as expert** — "You would know better than anyone..."
5. **Be curious, not knowing** — ask genuine questions, not interpretations
6. **Document preferred stories** — offer summary letters
7. **Avoid why-questions** — "Why" invites self-blame. Use "How" and "What."
