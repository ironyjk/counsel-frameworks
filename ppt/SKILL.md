# Positive Psychotherapy (PPT)

> "Well-being is not just about the absence of misery and suffering. It is about having meaning, good relationships, and accomplishment." — Martin Seligman

> "What if therapy could help people flourish, not just stop suffering?" — Tayyab Rashid

## Overview

Positive Psychotherapy (PPT) integrates positive psychology science into a structured therapeutic framework. Developed by Martin Seligman and Tayyab Rashid, PPT does not ignore suffering — it treats it while simultaneously building the pillars of well-being. The core premise: reducing negative symptoms is necessary but insufficient; building positive resources creates lasting resilience and flourishing.

PPT is distinct from "positive thinking." It does not dismiss pain or paste optimism over real problems. It works WITH suffering while also building what makes life worth living.

---

## PERMA Model

The five pillars of well-being. Each is independently measurable, independently pursuable, and intrinsically valued.

### P — Positive Emotion

Experiencing joy, gratitude, serenity, interest, hope, pride, amusement, inspiration, awe, and love.

- Not about being happy all the time — about broadening the ratio of positive to negative emotions
- Barbara Fredrickson's broaden-and-build theory: positive emotions expand awareness and build lasting resources
- **Target ratio**: 3:1 positive to negative emotional experiences (Fredrickson & Losada)

**Key exercises**:
- Three Good Things (see below)
- Savoring — deliberately attending to and prolonging positive experiences
- Gratitude Letter and Gratitude Visit

### E — Engagement

Deep absorption in activities — flow states where time disappears and self-consciousness dissolves.

- Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi's flow: challenge-skill balance, clear goals, immediate feedback
- Engagement comes from deploying your signature strengths in daily life
- Not about pleasure (hedonic) — about being fully alive (eudaimonic)

**Key exercises**:
- Identify signature strengths (VIA Survey)
- Use top strengths in a new way each day
- Find flow activities: What makes time disappear for you?

### R — Relationships

Positive relationships with others — the single strongest predictor of well-being across all studies.

- Active Constructive Responding (ACR): How you respond to good news matters more than how you handle bad news
- Christopher Peterson's summary of positive psychology in three words: "Other people matter."

**Active Constructive Responding styles**:

| Response Style | Example (to "I got a promotion!") | Effect |
|---------------|----------------------------------|--------|
| Active Constructive | "That's wonderful! Tell me everything — how did you find out? How do you feel?" | Builds relationship |
| Passive Constructive | "That's nice." | Missed opportunity |
| Active Destructive | "More responsibility? Are you sure you can handle it?" | Damages relationship |
| Passive Destructive | "Let me tell you about MY day." | Damages relationship |

### M — Meaning

Belonging to and serving something larger than the self.

- Purpose, cause, faith, community, legacy
- Viktor Frankl: meaning can be found even in suffering
- Three paths to meaning: purpose (goals beyond self), values (what you stand for), efficacy (you matter)

**Key exercises**:
- Write your legacy statement: What do you want to be remembered for?
- Identify where you serve something beyond yourself
- Connect daily tasks to larger purpose

### A — Accomplishment

Pursuing achievement, mastery, and competence for its own sake.

- Not external validation — intrinsic drive toward mastery
- Angela Duckworth's grit: passion + perseverance for long-term goals
- Growth mindset (Dweck): ability is developed, not fixed

**Key exercises**:
- Set and track meaningful goals (process-focused, not just outcome)
- Celebrate small wins — accomplishment breeds accomplishment
- Reflect on past achievements as evidence of capability

## VIA Character Strengths (24 Strengths)

Peterson & Seligman's classification of human virtues. Everyone has all 24; the top 5-7 are "signature strengths."

### The 6 Virtues and 24 Strengths

| Virtue | Strengths |
|--------|-----------|
| **Wisdom** | Creativity, Curiosity, Judgment/Critical Thinking, Love of Learning, Perspective |
| **Courage** | Bravery, Perseverance, Honesty, Zest |
| **Humanity** | Love, Kindness, Social Intelligence |
| **Justice** | Teamwork, Fairness, Leadership |
| **Temperance** | Forgiveness, Humility, Prudence, Self-Regulation |
| **Transcendence** | Appreciation of Beauty, Gratitude, Hope, Humor, Spirituality |

### Using Strengths in PPT

1. **Identify** signature strengths (VIA Survey at viacharacter.org)
2. **Use top strengths in new ways** each day for one week
3. **Reframe problems** through the lens of strengths: "How can my [strength] help me with this?"
4. **Signature strengths feel**: authentic ("this is the real me"), energizing (not draining), and natural (easy to deploy)

## Core Exercises

### 1. Three Good Things (Three Blessings)

Every night for at least one week, write down three things that went well today and WHY they went well.

**Format**:
1. What happened? (specific event)
2. Why did this good thing happen? (attribution — internal, stable, global causes preferred)
3. What does this mean to you?

**Why it works**: Shifts attentional bias from threat-scanning to benefit-finding. The "why" component builds explanatory style toward internal, stable attributions.

**Research**: Seligman et al. (2005) — participants showed increased happiness and decreased depression for six months after one week of practice.

### 2. Gratitude Letter and Visit

Write a detailed letter of gratitude to someone who was kind to you and whom you never properly thanked. Then read it aloud to them in person.

**Steps**:
1. Choose someone specific who made a real difference
2. Write 300+ words about what they did, how it affected you, and what it means now
3. Arrange a visit (don't reveal the purpose)
4. Read the letter aloud, face to face
5. Discuss together

**Research**: One of the strongest happiness-boosting exercises in the literature. Effects last 1-3 months.

### 3. Best Possible Self

Write about your life in the future where everything has gone as well as it possibly could. You worked hard and achieved all your goals.

**Instructions**:
- Write for 20 minutes without editing
- Cover career, relationships, health, personal growth
- Be specific and vivid — not vague ideals
- Focus on what IS (positive vision) not what ISN'T (problems solved)

**Why it works**: Clarifies goals, increases optimism, builds motivation through mental simulation.

### 4. Signature Strengths in New Ways

Each day for one week, use one of your top 5 character strengths in a new and different way.

**Examples**:
- Curiosity: Learn something about a colleague you didn't know
- Kindness: Do an anonymous act of service
- Humor: Find levity in a tense meeting
- Perseverance: Tackle a task you've been avoiding, bit by bit

### 5. Savoring Exercise

Once a day, take 10 minutes to fully attend to a positive experience. Use all senses. Don't rush, don't multitask, don't photograph it.

**Savoring strategies**: Sharing with others, memory building, self-congratulation, sharpening perception, absorption.

## 15-Session Structure

The Rashid & Seligman PPT protocol (adapted):

| Session | Focus | Key Activity |
|---------|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Orientation | Introduce PPT. Discuss presenting concerns AND strengths. Assign: Positive Introduction (story of a time you were at your best) |
| 2 | Character Strengths | Review VIA results. Identify signature strengths. Assign: Use top strength in a new way daily |
| 3 | Signature Strengths in Practice | Debrief strength use. Discuss fit and authenticity. Assign: continued practice |
| 4 | Good vs. Bad Memories | Explore impact of negative bias. Introduce Three Good Things. Assign: Three Good Things nightly |
| 5 | Forgiveness | Explore grudges and their cost. Discuss forgiveness as a strength (not condoning). Assign: write a forgiveness letter (don't send) |
| 6 | Gratitude | Explore gratitude as a trait and practice. Assign: Gratitude Letter |
| 7 | Gratitude Visit | Debrief the visit experience. Discuss mid-therapy progress |
| 8 | Satisficing vs. Maximizing | Discuss Barry Schwartz's paradox of choice. Good enough vs. best possible. Assign: practice satisficing in one domain |
| 9 | Hope and Optimism | Explanatory style (Seligman). Disputing pessimistic thoughts. Assign: ABCDE exercise for pessimistic explanations |
| 10 | Positive Communication | Active Constructive Responding. Assign: practice ACR with one key person daily |
| 11 | Signature Strengths of Others | Recognize strengths in family/friends. Assign: strength-spot 3 people this week |
| 12 | Meaning and Purpose | Craft a meaning narrative. Connect strengths to something larger. Assign: write legacy statement |
| 13 | Best Possible Self | Write and discuss. Bridge to concrete goals |
| 14 | Slow Positive Experiences | Savoring. Full life vs. empty life. Assign: one savoring experience daily |
| 15 | Full Life Review | Integration. Review progress across PERMA. Relapse prevention. What will you continue? |

## Example Dialogue

**Client**: "I feel like nothing I do matters. I go to work, come home, repeat. What's the point?"

**Therapist**: "I hear that — a sense of emptiness, like the routine has drained the meaning out of things. Before we explore that further, I want to ask: when was the last time you felt genuinely absorbed in something? Where time just flew by?"

**Client**: "Maybe... when I was helping my nephew with his science project last month. We were building a volcano and I lost track of time."

**Therapist**: "Tell me more about that. What was happening for you in that moment?"

**Client**: "I don't know, I was just... into it. Figuring out the chemistry, watching his face light up when it erupted."

**Therapist**: "So you were engaged — using your curiosity and your love of learning. And it sounds like the connection with your nephew added something too."

**Client**: "Yeah, I guess it felt like it mattered."

**Therapist**: "That's important data. The emptiness you feel at work — what if part of what's missing is engagement and connection? Not that something is wrong with you, but that certain wells haven't been tapped. What would it look like to bring more of that curiosity and connection into your week?"

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Toxic positivity** — "Just focus on the positive!" PPT does NOT ignore suffering. It treats distress AND builds well-being simultaneously.
2. **Dismissing real problems** — Strengths work complements, not replaces, addressing genuine issues like depression, anxiety, or trauma.
3. **Forcing gratitude** — Gratitude must be authentic. Manufactured gratitude backfires. Meet people where they are.
4. **Strength blindness** — Ignoring weaknesses entirely. PPT uses strengths as a lever, not a blindfold.
5. **Hedonic treadmill confusion** — PPT is eudaimonic (meaning, engagement, mastery) not purely hedonic (pleasure-seeking).
6. **One-size-fits-all** — Not every exercise fits every person. Customize based on the individual's profile and readiness.
7. **Skipping the negative** — PPT sessions should address distress first, then build positive. Ignoring pain to chase "flourishing" is harmful.
8. **Treating PERMA as a checklist** — PERMA is a framework for understanding well-being, not a to-do list.

---

## Scope and Limitations

- PPT is a learning and self-reflection framework in this context, not a substitute for professional therapy
- Not appropriate as sole intervention for severe depression, acute crisis, or psychosis
- Works best alongside appropriate clinical treatment when clinical issues are present
- The 15-session protocol is an educational reference; professional supervision is needed for clinical application
