# Solution-Focused Brief Therapy (SFBT)

> "If it ain't broke, don't fix it. If it works, do more of it. If it doesn't work, do something different." — Steve de Shazer

> "The problem is not the problem; coping is the problem." — Insoo Kim Berg

## Overview

SFBT is a goal-directed, future-focused therapeutic approach developed by Steve de Shazer and Insoo Kim Berg at the Brief Family Therapy Center (Milwaukee). Instead of analyzing the origins and pathology of problems, SFBT identifies what is already working and builds on existing strengths and solutions. Therapy is brief (typically 1-5 sessions) and driven by the client's own vision of their preferred future.

## Core Assumptions

1. **Change is constant and inevitable** — the question is not IF change happens, but how to direct it
2. **Clients are the experts** on their own lives and already possess the resources to solve their problems
3. **There is no necessary connection** between a problem and its solution — you don't need to understand the cause to find the solution
4. **Small changes lead to larger changes** — a tiny shift in one area cascades through the system
5. **If something works, do more of it** — regardless of whether it "should" work in theory
6. **If something doesn't work, do something different** — anything different
7. **The solution is not necessarily related to the problem** — solutions come from what works, not from analyzing what doesn't

## The Miracle Question

The signature SFBT intervention. It invites the client to describe their preferred future in vivid, concrete detail.

### Standard Formulation

> "I'm going to ask you a strange question now. Suppose tonight, while you're sleeping, a miracle happens. The miracle is that the problem that brought you here is solved. But you don't know it's happened because you're asleep. When you wake up tomorrow morning, what would be the first small sign that tells you something is different — that the miracle has happened?"

### Key Follow-Ups

1. **"What else?"** — Keep expanding the picture. Ask at least 3-5 times.
2. **"Who would notice first?"** — Brings in relational perspective.
3. **"What would [partner/friend/colleague] see you doing differently?"** — Externalizes the change.
4. **"On a scale of 1-10, where 10 is the miracle and 1 is the worst, where are you today?"** — Bridges to scaling.
5. **"What parts of the miracle are already happening, even a little?"** — Connects to exceptions.

### Purpose

The miracle question does NOT ask "what would be different if the problem were gone." It asks what life would look like on the OTHER side. This distinction matters — it shifts from problem-elimination to future-construction.

## Scaling Questions

Scaling questions make the abstract concrete and trackable.

### Basic Scaling

> "On a scale of 1 to 10, where 10 is [goal state] and 1 is [worst state], where are you right now?"

### Strategic Follow-Ups

| Follow-Up | Purpose |
|-----------|---------|
| "What puts you at a [N] and not a 1?" | Elicit strengths and resources (what's already working) |
| "What would a [N+1] look like?" | Define the next small step (not the whole staircase) |
| "What would others say you're at?" | Introduce relational perspective |
| "When were you last at a [N+1]?" | Find exceptions |
| "What did you do to get from [lower] to [current]?" | Highlight client agency |

### Varieties

- **Confidence scaling**: "How confident are you that you can get to [N+1]?"
- **Willingness scaling**: "How willing are you to do what it takes?"
- **Relationship scaling**: "Where would your partner put you on this scale?"
- **Progress scaling**: "Compared to when we first talked, where are you now?"

## Exception Questions

Exceptions are times when the problem COULD have occurred but DIDN'T. They contain the DNA of solutions.

### Core Exception Questions

1. **"When was the last time [the problem] didn't happen?"**
2. **"Tell me about a time recently when things were even a little better."**
3. **"What was different about that time?"**
4. **"What were YOU doing differently?"**
5. **"How did you make that happen?"** (presupposes agency)
6. **"What would it take to do that again?"**

### Types of Exceptions

| Type | Description | Response |
|------|-------------|----------|
| Deliberate | Client consciously did something different | Amplify and repeat |
| Random/Spontaneous | It just happened | Help client take ownership: "What part of that can you recreate?" |
| Relational | Someone else behaved differently | Explore: "What did you do that invited that response?" |

## Coping Questions

Used when the client sees NO exceptions and feels hopeless. These questions acknowledge the difficulty while highlighting hidden resilience.

> "Given everything you've been dealing with, how have you managed to keep going?"
> "What has kept you from giving up entirely?"
> "How do you get through each day?"
> "That sounds incredibly hard. What strength are you drawing on to be here today?"

Coping questions presuppose competence in the face of adversity. They don't minimize pain — they honor survival.

## EARS Process

The SFBT session engine. Use this cycle throughout each session:

### E — Elicit

Draw out what is working, exceptions, client resources, and preferred futures.
- "What's been better since last time?"
- "What have you noticed that's different?"
- "Tell me about a time this week when things went well."

### A — Amplify

Expand on what the client reports. Get details, sequence, sensory information.
- "Tell me more about that."
- "What exactly did you do?"
- "Who noticed? What did they say?"
- "How did that feel?"

### R — Reinforce

Acknowledge and validate the change. Communicate that it matters.
- "That took real effort."
- "So you figured out a way to handle that differently."
- "That's significant — you chose to respond rather than react."

### S — Start Again

Return to Elicit. Look for the next exception, the next resource, the next small success.
- "What else has been better?"
- "What else would you like to see different?"

## Session Structure

### Session 1: Problem-Free Talk + Miracle Question

1. **Problem-free talk** (5 min): Build rapport by talking about strengths, not problems. "What are you good at?" "What do you enjoy?"
2. **Problem description** (brief): Let them describe the issue, but don't dwell. "What brings you here today?"
3. **Miracle question** (15-20 min): Full exploration of preferred future.
4. **Exception finding** (10 min): "When is the miracle already happening, even a little?"
5. **Scaling** (5 min): "Where are you now on a 1-10 scale?"
6. **Compliment + task** (5 min): Genuine compliment on strengths observed. Between-session task.

### Sessions 2-5: EARS-Based

1. **"What's better?"** — The opening question of every follow-up session. NOT "How was your week?" but "What's been better since last time?"
2. **EARS cycle** on whatever they report
3. **Scaling** to track progress
4. **Compliment + next task**

### Ending Therapy

SFBT is brief by design. Typical: 3-5 sessions. End when:
- Client reports being at their goal on the scale
- Client says they have enough tools to continue on their own
- No further sessions needed — celebrate and close

## Compliments

Compliments in SFBT are deliberate, genuine observations about client strengths and resources.

**Direct compliments**: "You showed real creativity in how you handled that."
**Indirect compliments** (embedded in questions): "How did you figure out how to do that?" (presupposes they figured it out)

### Rules for SFBT Compliments

1. Base them on what the client actually said or did (not generic praise)
2. Connect them to the client's goals
3. Highlight agency: what THEY did, not what happened to them
4. Use them to bridge to the between-session task

---

## Between-Session Tasks

### Three Standard Tasks

**1. Observation task** (for clients who are visitors/complainants):
> "Between now and next time, I'd like you to notice what happens in your life that you'd like to continue."

**2. Do more task** (for clients with identified exceptions):
> "You mentioned that [exception]. Between now and next time, do more of that."

**3. Pretend task** (for clients stuck in problem-talk):
> "Pick one day this week and pretend the miracle has happened. Notice what's different."

---

## Client Relationship Types

| Type | Description | Intervention |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| **Visitor** | Doesn't see a problem; referred by others | Compliment. Observation task. No direct change task. |
| **Complainant** | Sees a problem but locates it in others | Observation task. Exception questions about their own role. |
| **Customer** | Sees a problem and is willing to work on it | Full SFBT toolkit. Exception + do more tasks. |

Adjust your approach based on the relationship type. Never force "customer" interventions on a "visitor."

## Example Dialogue

**Client**: "I just can't stop arguing with my teenager. Every conversation turns into a fight."

**Therapist**: "That sounds exhausting. Let me ask — when was the last time you and your teenager had a conversation that went even a little better than usual?"

**Client**: "Hmm... Actually, last Sunday we were cooking together and it was okay."

**Therapist**: "Tell me more about that. What was different about Sunday?" *(Amplify)*

**Client**: "I think... I wasn't trying to lecture. We were just making pasta and talking about the recipe."

**Therapist**: "So when you weren't in lecture mode, when you were side-by-side doing something together, the conversation flowed naturally." *(Reinforce)*

**Client**: "Yeah, I guess when I'm not trying to FIX her, we get along fine."

**Therapist**: "That's a really important insight. On a scale of 1 to 10, where 10 is every conversation going like that Sunday pasta night, where are you now?"

**Client**: "Maybe a 3."

**Therapist**: "What makes it a 3 and not a 1?"

**Client**: "Well, we do have some good moments. And I know she loves me."

**Therapist**: "Between now and our next meeting, I'd like you to notice the moments that feel like Sunday — even briefly. Just notice what's happening in those moments."

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Problem archaeology** — Digging into the history and causes of the problem. SFBT builds futures, not excavates pasts.
2. **Why questions** — "Why do you think you do that?" invites pathology. Replace with "How have you managed despite that?"
3. **Expert prescriptions** — Telling clients what to do. SFBT elicits solutions FROM clients.
4. **Forcing the miracle question** — If the client isn't ready or is too distressed, use coping questions first.
5. **Ignoring real danger** — SFBT is not appropriate when immediate safety is at risk. Assess for suicidality, abuse, and crisis first.
6. **Dismissing pain** — Solution-focus does NOT mean ignoring suffering. Acknowledge it fully before redirecting to resources.
7. **Asking "Why not higher?"** on scaling — This evokes deficit-talk. Always ask "Why not lower?" to elicit strengths.
8. **Treating visitors as customers** — Pushing change tasks on someone who doesn't see a problem guarantees dropout.

---

## Scope and Limitations

- SFBT is a learning framework, not a substitute for professional therapy
- Not appropriate for acute crisis, active psychosis, or severe trauma without stabilization
- Works best when the client has some baseline stability and can envision a preferred future
- This skill provides educational content about the SFBT model for self-reflection and guided conversation
