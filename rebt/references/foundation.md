# REBT — Background and Theory

> Foundation reference for Rational Emotive Behavior Therapy. Loaded on demand.

## Origins

REBT, developed by Albert Ellis in 1955, is the original cognitive-behavioral therapy. It holds that emotional disturbance is caused not by adverse events, but by the irrational beliefs we hold about those events. REBT is distinguished by its directness, its emphasis on unconditional self-acceptance, and its philosophical depth rooted in Stoicism.

Where CBT focuses on distorted automatic thoughts, REBT targets rigid, absolutistic demands ("musts" and "shoulds") as the root of psychological disturbance.

## The 11 Irrational Beliefs (Ellis's Classic List)

### Category 1: Demandingness (Musturbation)
The root of all irrational beliefs. Three forms:

1. **Demands on self:** "I MUST do well and win approval, or I am a worthless person"
2. **Demands on others:** "Others MUST treat me fairly and kindly, or they are rotten people"
3. **Demands on the world:** "Conditions MUST be the way I want them, or life is unbearable"

### Category 2: Derivatives of Demandingness

4. **Catastrophizing (Awfulizing):** Rating an event as 100% bad, worse than it could possibly be
5. **Low Frustration Tolerance (LFT):** Believing you literally cannot endure discomfort
6. **Global Evaluation of Self:** Rating your entire self based on one behavior
7. **Global Evaluation of Others:** Damning the entire person for one action

### Category 3: Specific Common Irrational Beliefs

8. **Love/approval dependency:** "I MUST be loved by virtually every significant person"
9. **Perfectionism:** "I MUST be thoroughly competent in all important respects"
10. **Blame and damnation:** "People who harm me are BAD and should be severely punished"
11. **Catastrophic expectations:** "If something is dangerous, I should be terribly upset about it"

## REBT's Three Core Philosophies of Acceptance

### 1. Unconditional Self-Acceptance (USA)
- You are neither good nor bad — you are a complex, fallible human being
- Your worth is not determined by achievements, approval, or behaviors
- "I did a bad thing" is rational. "I am a bad person" is irrational.

### 2. Unconditional Other-Acceptance (UOA)
- Other people are also complex, fallible human beings
- "She acted unfairly" is rational. "She is a terrible person" is irrational.

### 3. Unconditional Life-Acceptance (ULA)
- Life is not fair, and it does not have to be
- "This situation is unfortunate" is rational. "This is unbearable" is irrational.

## Shame-Attacking Exercises

A signature REBT technique. Deliberately do something mildly embarrassing in public and practice tolerating the discomfort.

**Rules:** Must NOT harm yourself or others. Must NOT be illegal. MUST be genuinely uncomfortable. Must be in a real social context.

**Examples:** Sing loudly on a busy street. Wear mismatched shoes. Ask for a discount where none exists.

**Processing:** What happened? How bad was it vs. your prediction? Did you survive? What does this teach about discomfort vs. catastrophe?

## Rational Coping Statements

- "I strongly prefer to succeed, but I don't HAVE to. I can still be a worthwhile person even if I fail."
- "It's unfortunate that they treated me unfairly. I don't like it, but I can stand it."
- "This is bad, but it's not the worst thing that could ever happen."
- "I am a fallible human being. Fallible humans make mistakes."
- "Discomfort is not the same as danger."

## REBT vs. CBT: Key Differences

| Dimension | REBT | CBT |
|-----------|------|-----|
| Primary target | Rigid demands ("musts") | Distorted automatic thoughts |
| Philosophical depth | Explicitly Stoic, existential | More pragmatic, evidence-focused |
| Therapist style | Direct, sometimes confrontational | Collaborative, Socratic |
| Self-acceptance | Unconditional (rate behavior, never self) | Conditional (build positive self-schema) |
| Goal | Healthy negative emotions + high frustration tolerance | Reduction of symptoms |
