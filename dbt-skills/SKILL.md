# DBT Skills Training

> "You can't stop the waves, but you can learn to surf." — Jon Kabat-Zinn (often cited in DBT context)

> "Dialectics means two things that seem opposite can both be true at the same time." — Marsha Linehan

## Overview

Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) Skills Training teaches concrete, practical skills for managing intense emotions, tolerating distress, improving relationships, and staying present. Developed by Marsha Linehan initially for borderline personality disorder, these skills have proven effective across a wide range of emotional difficulties.

**Scope limitation**: This skill covers the **skills training** component of DBT only. Full DBT is a comprehensive treatment system with four modes: individual therapy, skills training group, phone coaching, and therapist consultation team. This skill addresses skills training alone — it is an educational resource, not a therapeutic intervention.

The core dialectic: **Acceptance AND Change**. You must fully accept where you are AND work to change. Not one or the other — both simultaneously.

## Module 1: Mindfulness (Core Mindfulness)

The foundation of all other DBT skills. Every other module builds on mindfulness.

### Wise Mind

The synthesis of Emotion Mind and Reasonable Mind.

```
     Emotion Mind          Reasonable Mind
     (feelings rule)       (logic rules)
           \                   /
            \                 /
             \               /
              \             /
               Wise Mind
          (feelings + logic + intuition)
```

- **Emotion Mind**: Decisions driven entirely by feelings. Hot, reactive, impulsive.
- **Reasonable Mind**: Decisions driven entirely by logic. Cold, detached, intellectual.
- **Wise Mind**: The integration. Knows what feels right AND makes sense. Accessed through practice, not force.

**Accessing Wise Mind**:
- Ask: "What does my wise mind say about this?"
- Slow down. Breathe. Drop your attention to your center (belly, chest).
- It often arrives as a quiet knowing, not a loud proclamation.

### "What" Skills

| Skill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Observe** | Notice without getting caught up. Watch thoughts like clouds. Notice sensations without reacting. |
| **Describe** | Put words on what you observe. "I'm having the thought that..." / "I notice a tightness in my chest." |
| **Participate** | Throw yourself fully into the current activity. One-mindfully. No self-consciousness. |

### "How" Skills

| Skill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Non-judgmentally** | Describe facts, not evaluations. "I didn't finish the report" not "I'm a failure." Replace "should" with "I'd prefer." |
| **One-mindfully** | One thing at a time. Full attention on the current activity. When mind wanders, bring it back. |
| **Effectively** | Do what works in this situation. Let go of "right" vs. "wrong" and focus on your goals. Don't let pride or principles sabotage effectiveness. |

## Module 2: Distress Tolerance

Skills for surviving a crisis WITHOUT making it worse. These are not solutions — they are survival tools for the acute moment.

### TIPP — Change Your Body Chemistry Fast

For when emotions are at 7+ on a 0-10 scale and you need immediate physiological relief.

| Letter | Skill | How |
|--------|-------|-----|
| **T** | Temperature | Cold water on face (dive reflex), ice on wrists/neck. Triggers mammalian dive reflex, drops heart rate. **Note: The cold water technique may be contraindicated for cardiac conditions. Consult a physician if you have heart-related health concerns.** |
| **I** | Intense exercise | 10-20 minutes of vigorous movement. Burns off adrenaline and cortisol. |
| **P** | Paced breathing | Exhale longer than inhale (e.g., 4 in, 6 out). Activates parasympathetic nervous system. |
| **P** | Paired muscle relaxation | Tense each muscle group while inhaling, release while exhaling and saying "relax." |

### STOP — Before You Act Impulsively

| Letter | Skill |
|--------|-------|
| **S** | Stop. Freeze. Don't move. Don't react. |
| **T** | Take a step back. Breathe. Disengage physically if needed. |
| **O** | Observe. What's happening inside you? Outside you? What are the facts? |
| **P** | Proceed mindfully. Act from Wise Mind, not Emotion Mind. |

### Distract with Wise Mind ACCEPTS

| Letter | Skill | Example |
|--------|-------|---------|
| **A** | Activities | Do something engaging — walk, clean, puzzle, cook |
| **C** | Contributing | Help someone else. Volunteer. Do a kindness. |
| **C** | Comparisons | Compare to a harder time you survived. Compare to those less fortunate (with compassion). |
| **E** | Emotions (opposite) | Watch a comedy when sad. Listen to calm music when agitated. |
| **P** | Pushing away | Mentally put the problem in a box and put it on a shelf — temporarily. |
| **T** | Thoughts | Count to 10. Do a word puzzle. Recite song lyrics. Occupy the cognitive channel. |
| **S** | Sensations | Hold ice. Snap a rubber band. Strong mint. Cold shower. Intense but safe physical stimulus. |

### Radical Acceptance

The cornerstone of distress tolerance. Accepting reality as it IS, not as you want it to be.

**Radical acceptance is**:
- Acknowledging what IS, without approving or liking it
- Letting go of the fight against reality
- Accepting with your whole being — mind, body, spirit

**Radical acceptance is NOT**:
- Approval or agreement
- Giving up or being passive
- Forgiveness (that may come later)
- Pretending it doesn't hurt

**The formula**: **Pain + Non-acceptance = Suffering**. Pain is inevitable. Suffering is pain multiplied by resistance.

**Practice**:
1. Notice you are fighting reality ("This shouldn't be happening")
2. Remind yourself: "This IS happening. Fighting reality doesn't change it."
3. Accept with your body: unclench jaw, drop shoulders, open hands
4. Practice half-smile and willing hands (physical cues that signal acceptance to the brain)

### Turning the Mind

Radical acceptance is not a one-time decision. It must be made again and again. When you notice you've turned away from acceptance, gently turn back. This is not failure — this IS the practice.

## Module 3: Emotion Regulation

Skills for understanding, reducing vulnerability to, and changing unwanted emotions.

### PLEASE — Reduce Vulnerability to Emotion Mind

Take care of the basics. Emotional vulnerability increases when physical health deteriorates.

| Letter | Skill |
|--------|-------|
| **PL** | Treat Physical iLlness — see a doctor, take medication, attend to health |
| **E** | Balanced Eating — regular meals, adequate nutrition, avoid mood-altering foods |
| **A** | Avoid mood-Altering substances — alcohol, drugs, excessive caffeine |
| **S** | Balance Sleep — consistent schedule, sleep hygiene, 7-9 hours |
| **E** | Get Exercise — 20+ minutes of movement most days |

### Check the Facts

Before acting on an emotion, check whether it fits the facts of the situation.

**Steps**:
1. **What is the emotion?** Name it specifically.
2. **What is the prompting event?** What triggered it? (Observable facts only)
3. **What are my interpretations?** Am I adding meaning that isn't in the facts?
4. **Am I assuming threat?** What's the actual probability of the feared outcome?
5. **Does the intensity fit the facts?** Is this a 2-level problem getting a 9-level response?
6. **Does the emotion fit the facts?** (If yes, problem-solve. If no, act opposite.)

### Opposite Action

When an emotion does NOT fit the facts (or fits but acting on it would be harmful), do the OPPOSITE of what the emotion urges.

| Emotion | Urge | Opposite Action |
|---------|------|----------------|
| Fear (unjustified) | Avoid, run, hide | Approach. Do the thing. Stay present. |
| Anger (unjustified) | Attack, lash out | Gently avoid. Be kind. Take space calmly. |
| Sadness (unjustified) | Withdraw, isolate | Get active. Reach out to someone. Engage. |
| Shame (unjustified) | Hide, shrink | Share with a trusted person. Hold your head up. |
| Guilt (unjustified) | Over-apologize, punish self | Don't apologize again. Act consistently with values. |

**Key**: Opposite action only works when done ALL THE WAY. Half-hearted opposite action reinforces the original emotion.

### Build Mastery

Do one thing each day that builds a sense of competence and achievement. Plan it in advance. It should be slightly challenging but achievable. Over time, this builds a foundation of confidence.

### Build Positive Experiences (Short-Term and Long-Term)

- **Short-term**: Do one pleasant thing each day. Be mindful during it. Don't worry about "deserving" it.
- **Long-term**: Identify your values. Set goals aligned with those values. Take one step toward a goal. Attend to relationships.

## Module 4: Interpersonal Effectiveness

Skills for getting what you need in relationships while maintaining self-respect and the relationship itself.

### DEAR MAN — Getting What You Want

Use when you need to ask for something or say no.

| Letter | Skill | Example |
|--------|-------|---------|
| **D** | Describe | State the facts of the situation. No judgments. "We agreed to meet at 7, and I arrived at 7. You arrived at 7:45." |
| **E** | Express | Share how you feel about it. "I felt worried and frustrated." |
| **A** | Assert | Ask clearly for what you want OR say no clearly. "I'd like you to text me if you're going to be late." |
| **R** | Reinforce | Explain the positive consequences. "That way I won't worry, and we can enjoy our time together." |
| **M** | Stay Mindful | Broken record: keep returning to your request. Don't get sidetracked by attacks or tangents. |
| **A** | Appear confident | Posture, eye contact, steady voice. Even if you don't feel it, act it. |
| **N** | Negotiate | Be willing to give to get. "What would work for you?" Offer alternatives. |

### GIVE — Maintaining the Relationship

Use when the relationship matters more than the specific outcome.

| Letter | Skill |
|--------|-------|
| **G** | Be Gentle — no attacks, threats, or judgments. No "you always" / "you never." |
| **I** | Act Interested — listen. Ask questions. Don't interrupt. Face them. |
| **V** | Validate — acknowledge their feelings and perspective. "I can see why you'd feel that way." |
| **E** | Use an Easy manner — lighten up. Use humor where appropriate. Smile. Be soft. |

### FAST — Maintaining Self-Respect

Use when your integrity and self-respect are at stake.

| Letter | Skill |
|--------|-------|
| **F** | Be Fair — to yourself AND the other person. |
| **A** | No Apologies (unnecessary ones) — don't apologize for having a need, an opinion, or for existing. |
| **S** | Stick to values — don't sell out your principles for short-term peace. |
| **T** | Be Truthful — don't lie, exaggerate, or manipulate. |

### Balancing Priorities

In any interpersonal situation, three goals compete:

1. **Objective effectiveness** (DEAR MAN): Getting what you want
2. **Relationship effectiveness** (GIVE): Keeping the relationship
3. **Self-respect effectiveness** (FAST): Maintaining your integrity

You must decide which matters most in THIS situation and weight your skills accordingly.

## The Dialectical Worldview

DBT skills rest on dialectical philosophy. Key dialectics:

| Thesis | Antithesis | Synthesis |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| I need to accept myself | I need to change | I can fully accept who I am AND work toward growth |
| I'm doing the best I can | I need to do better | Both are true at the same time |
| My emotions are valid | My emotions aren't facts | I can honor my feelings AND check the facts |
| I need independence | I need connection | I can be autonomous within relationships |

**The core dialectic of DBT**: You are doing the best you can, AND you need to try harder. Both. Simultaneously.

## Example Dialogue

**Person**: "My boss criticized my work in front of the whole team. I wanted to scream at him. I didn't, but I went to the bathroom and punched the wall."

**Skills guide**: "So in that moment, the anger was really intense — maybe an 8 or 9. And you had the urge to scream, which you managed to hold back. That took some skill. The wall-punching tells me you needed a physical outlet. Let's look at what might work better next time."

**Person**: "I just can't stand being humiliated."

**Skills guide**: "That's a really painful experience. Let me ask — let's check the facts for a moment. What did your boss actually say?"

**Person**: "He said the report had errors and I needed to redo section 3."

**Skills guide**: "Okay. And the emotion was humiliation and anger. Let's separate: was the criticism factual? Did section 3 have errors?"

**Person**: "...Yeah, it did. But he didn't have to say it in front of everyone."

**Skills guide**: "Right. So there are two things here. The content — which was accurate — and the delivery — which felt public and humiliating. For the delivery piece, that sounds like a DEAR MAN conversation with your boss privately. For the emotion in the moment — next time, what if you tried TIPP? Cold water on your face in that bathroom instead of the wall. Drops the physiological intensity within 30 seconds."

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Using skills to avoid feeling** — Skills manage emotions, not eliminate them. Feeling is not the enemy.
2. **Treating this as full DBT** — This is skills training only. Full DBT includes individual therapy, phone coaching, and therapist consultation. Do not present skills training as equivalent to comprehensive DBT treatment.
3. **Radical acceptance as resignation** — Acceptance is not giving up. It's the foundation for effective action.
4. **Opposite action for justified emotions** — If the emotion fits the facts, the answer is problem-solving, not opposite action.
5. **DEAR MAN as a script** — It's a framework, not a word-for-word recipe. Adapt to the relationship and context.
6. **Skipping mindfulness** — Mindfulness is the foundation. Without Wise Mind awareness, other skills lose their anchor.
7. **All-or-nothing skill use** — Using skills 30% of the time is infinitely better than 0%. Progress, not perfection.
8. **Skill shaming** — "You should have used STOP!" Judging someone for not using skills defeats the purpose.

## Scope and Limitations

- This skill covers DBT skills training content ONLY — it is educational, not therapeutic
- Full DBT treatment (individual therapy, group skills training, phone coaching, consultation team) requires trained clinical providers
- Skills training alone is not sufficient for individuals with severe self-harm, suicidality, or personality disorders
- Individuals in acute crisis should seek professional help immediately
- This content is adapted from published materials for educational purposes
