# Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) Skill

> "The goal is not to feel better, but to get better at feeling." — Steven C. Hayes

## Overview

ACT (pronounced as the word "act," not the letters) is a third-wave behavioral therapy developed by Steven Hayes that uses acceptance and mindfulness strategies alongside commitment and behavior-change strategies to increase psychological flexibility. Instead of fighting unwanted thoughts and feelings, ACT teaches people to hold them lightly while moving toward what matters most.

## The Hexaflex Model: 6 Core Processes

ACT's model of psychological flexibility consists of six interconnected processes arranged in a hexagon. The goal is to move from rigidity (the "inflexaflex") toward flexibility in each process.

### 1. Acceptance (수용) — Open Up

**What it is:** Actively embracing private experiences (thoughts, feelings, sensations, urges) without trying to change their frequency or form.

**What it is NOT:** Resignation, tolerance, gritting your teeth, or passive giving up.

**Key distinction:**
- Control agenda: "I need to get rid of this anxiety before I can live my life"
- Acceptance: "I can have anxiety AND still do what matters to me"

**The Willingness Scale (0-10):**
Ask: "On a scale of 0 to 10, how willing are you to have this feeling — fully, without defense — if it meant you could move toward what matters?"
- 0 = completely unwilling
- 10 = fully willing, no struggle
- Target: Not 10. Honest awareness of where you are.

**Technique — Expansion:**
1. Notice where the feeling lives in your body
2. Observe its shape, size, color, temperature, texture
3. Breathe into that space — make room around it
4. Allow it to be there. You don't have to like it. Just let it be.

### 2. Cognitive Defusion (탈융합) — Watch Your Thinking

**What it is:** Changing your relationship to thoughts rather than changing the thoughts themselves. Seeing thoughts as what they are — strings of words, mental events — not what they say they are.

**Core technique — "I notice I'm having the thought that..."**
- Fused: "I'm a failure"
- Defused: "I notice I'm having the thought that I'm a failure"
- Further defused: "I notice my mind is telling me the 'failure' story again"

**Additional defusion techniques:**

| Technique | How To |
|-----------|--------|
| Repeat word rapidly | Say the feared word 30 times fast until it loses meaning |
| Silly voice | Hear the thought in a cartoon character's voice |
| Thank your mind | "Thank you, mind, for that interesting thought" |
| Name the story | "Ah, there's the 'I'm not good enough' story again" |
| Thought as object | "If this thought were an object, what would it look like?" |
| Leaves on a stream | Place each thought on a leaf floating down a stream |
| Passengers on the bus | (see detailed exercise below) |

**Passengers on the Bus Exercise:**
You are driving a bus toward your values. Passengers (thoughts, feelings, memories) get on. Some are loud, threatening, scary. They shout: "Turn back!" "You can't do this!" "Remember last time?"

The key insight: You are the driver. The passengers can shout, but they cannot steer. You can acknowledge them ("I hear you, Anxiety Passenger") and keep driving toward your values. The moment you stop the bus to argue with them, you stop moving.

### 3. Present Moment Contact (현재 순간 접촉) — Be Here Now

**What it is:** Flexible, voluntary, focused attention on the here-and-now experience.

**Why it matters:** Psychological suffering usually involves being stuck in the past (rumination) or the future (worry). The present moment is the only place where values-based action can occur.

**Quick grounding exercises:**
1. **5-4-3-2-1**: Name 5 things you see, 4 you hear, 3 you touch, 2 you smell, 1 you taste
2. **Feet on the floor**: Feel the pressure of your feet against the ground for 30 seconds
3. **Three breaths**: Three slow breaths, noticing the full journey of each one

### 4. Self-as-Context (맥락으로서의 자기) — The Observing Self

**What it is:** Contacting a transcendent sense of self — the "you" that has been there through every experience, observing but unchanged by the content of experience.

**Key distinction:**
- Self-as-content: "I AM anxious / depressed / a failure" (identity = experience)
- Self-as-context: "I am the space in which anxiety arises" (you are the sky; thoughts/feelings are weather)

**The Chessboard Metaphor:**
You are not the white pieces (positive thoughts) or the black pieces (negative thoughts). You are the board itself — the context that holds all the pieces. The pieces battle, but the board is not damaged.

### 5. Values (가치) — Know What Matters

**What it is:** Chosen life directions — not goals to achieve, but qualities of ongoing action. Values are like a compass heading: you never "arrive" at West, but you can always walk westward.

**Values vs Goals:**
- Value: "Being a loving partner"
- Goal: "Plan a date night this Friday"
- Value: "Continuous learning"
- Goal: "Read one book this month"

**Values Clarification Worksheet — 10 Life Domains:**

For each domain, rate: Importance (1-10) and Current consistency (1-10).

| # | Domain | Questions to Explore |
|---|--------|---------------------|
| 1 | Family relationships | What kind of sibling/child/parent do you want to be? |
| 2 | Intimate relationships | What qualities do you want to bring to partnership? |
| 3 | Parenting | What kind of parent do you want to be remembered as? |
| 4 | Friendships / social | What kind of friend do you want to be? |
| 5 | Career / work | What makes your work meaningful beyond money? |
| 6 | Education / growth | What do you want to learn and how do you want to grow? |
| 7 | Recreation / leisure | What activities bring you alive? |
| 8 | Spirituality / meaning | What gives your life a sense of purpose? |
| 9 | Community / citizenship | How do you want to contribute to the wider world? |
| 10 | Health / physical well-being | How do you want to care for your body? |

**The Sweet Spot:** Domains with HIGH importance but LOW consistency are the most fertile ground for committed action.

### 6. Committed Action (전념 행동) — Do What It Takes

**What it is:** Setting goals guided by values and carrying them out, with willingness to encounter psychological discomfort along the way.

**Committed Action Planning Template:**

```
Value: [chosen value]
Domain: [life domain]
Specific goal: [SMART — specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, time-bound]
Smallest possible step: [what you can do in the next 24 hours]
Barriers I expect: [thoughts, feelings, urges that will show up]
My willingness (0-10): [honest rating]
ACT response to barriers: [defusion/acceptance strategy]
```

## The Values Compass Exercise

When facing a decision or feeling stuck:

1. **STOP** — Pause and notice what is happening internally
2. **OBSERVE** — What thoughts, feelings, urges are present? (defusion)
3. **BREATHE** — Return to the present moment
4. **ASK** — "What does my values compass say? Which direction is toward what matters?"
5. **ACT** — Take one small step in that direction, carrying whatever shows up

## Choice Point Model (Russ Harris Adaptation)

A simplified ACT tool for any moment of difficulty:

```
         Situation / Hook
              |
         [CHOICE POINT]
            /       \
     AWAY moves    TOWARD moves
     (avoidance,    (values-based
      struggle,      action despite
      withdrawal)    discomfort)
```

Ask: "In this moment, am I moving TOWARD or AWAY from my values?"

## Example Dialogue

**Person:** "I keep procrastinating on starting my business. I think I'm just lazy and afraid of failure."

**ACT Response:**

"Let's slow down and notice what's happening here. Your mind is giving you two labels — 'lazy' and 'afraid.' These are stories your mind tells, and they sound painful.

Let me ask: if you could wave a magic wand and completely remove all fear of failure, would you still want to start this business?

[If yes]: So the desire is real. The value is there — maybe it's about growth, or contribution, or autonomy. What matters to you about starting this business?

Now, your mind shows up with 'you'll fail' — that's the Anxiety Passenger on your bus. It's been riding with you a long time. What if that passenger gets to stay on the bus, and you keep driving anyway?

What's the smallest step you could take toward this business in the next 48 hours — something so small that even with the fear present, you'd be willing to do it?"

## The Inflexaflex (Psychological Rigidity)

Each flexibility process has a rigid counterpart:

| Flexible Process | Rigid Counterpart |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| Acceptance | Experiential avoidance — running from discomfort |
| Defusion | Cognitive fusion — believing every thought |
| Present moment | Past/future dominance — rumination/worry |
| Self-as-context | Self-as-content — "I AM my thoughts/feelings" |
| Values | Disconnection — living on autopilot |
| Committed action | Inaction or impulsive action — stuck or reactive |

---

## Anti-patterns

1. **Using ACT to get rid of feelings** — "I'll accept my anxiety so it goes away." This is still the control agenda in disguise.
2. **Values as "shoulds"** — Values are freely chosen, not obligations. If it feels like duty, it may not be a value.
3. **Forcing willingness** — Willingness cannot be forced. You can only notice unwillingness honestly.
4. **Defusion as dismissal** — "It's just a thought" used to invalidate genuine concerns. Defusion is about holding lightly, not dismissing.
5. **Skipping acceptance for action** — Jumping to committed action without building acceptance first leads to white-knuckling.
6. **Mechanical application** — Reciting "I notice I'm having the thought that..." without genuine mindful awareness.
7. **Confusing values with goals** — "Get promoted" is a goal. "Being dedicated to excellent work" is a value.
8. **All-or-nothing committed action** — Setting huge goals then feeling like a failure. Start with the smallest possible step.
