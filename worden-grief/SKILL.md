# Worden's Grief Counseling

> "Mourning is not a state but a process, and an active one at that." — J. William Worden

> Background and theory: Read references/foundation.md

---

## The Four Tasks of Mourning

These are not sequential. They overlap, recur, and operate simultaneously.

### Task I: Accept the Reality of the Loss

Move from intellectual acknowledgment to deep emotional acceptance.

**What blocks:** Denial, mummification, minimization, geographic cure

**Techniques:**
- **Tell the story** — detailed narration of the death, repeated
- **Reality confrontation** — use "died" not euphemisms
- **Timeline work** — walk through events before and after

### Task II: Process the Pain of Grief

Experience the full range of pain rather than avoiding it.

**The pain:** Emotional (sadness, anger, guilt, yearning), physical (fatigue, chest tightness), cognitive (disbelief, confusion), behavioral (sleep disturbance, withdrawal)

**What blocks:** "Be strong" messages, substance use, constant busyness, idealization

**Techniques:**
- "What's the hardest part for you right now?"
- Normalize: "Many bereaved people feel angry — even at the person who died."
- Permission: "You don't have to be strong here."
- Empty chair: talk to the deceased

### Task III: Adjust to a World Without the Deceased

**External:** Practical roles (bills, cooking, decisions). New skills needed.
**Internal:** Identity shift. "Who am I without them?"
**Spiritual:** Meaning-making. "Why did this happen?"

**Techniques:**
- Problem-solving specific practical gaps
- Role exploration: "Who are you becoming?"
- Meaning-making: "What has this taught you about what matters?"

### Task IV: Find an Enduring Connection While Embarking on a New Life

Relocate the deceased emotionally — not "move on" or "let go."

**This IS:** Carrying them forward in a new way. Honoring memory while investing in new connections.
**This is NOT:** Forgetting. "Getting over it." Replacing.

**What blocks:** Guilt about new relationships. Grief as last connection. Premature pressure.

**Techniques:**
- **Continuing bonds ritual** — anniversary rituals, memory box, legacy project
- **Letter to deceased** — "Here's what's happened. Here's who I'm becoming."
- **Internalization** — "What did they teach you that lives on in you?"
- **Permission** — "Would they want you to live fully?"

## Example Dialogue

**Client**: "Everyone says I should be over this. It's been eight months."

**Therapist**: "Eight months. And people are telling you there's a timeline."

**Client**: "My sister said I need to move on. She suggested dating."

**Therapist**: "How did that land?"

**Client**: "Like a punch. I can't even open his closet."

**Therapist**: "The closet — what happens when you open it?"

**Client**: "I can smell him. His cologne. For a second he's here, and then... reality hits."

**Therapist**: "For one moment, he's present. Then the absence floods back. Those micro-moments are some of the most painful parts of grief."

**Client**: "Is something wrong with me?"

**Therapist**: "Nothing is wrong with you. You lost your partner of twenty-three years. That's not something you 'get over.' You learn to carry it differently. And you get to decide the pace."

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Timeline enforcement** — Grief has no schedule
2. **Stage-model rigidity** — Worden rejected stages; don't impose linear progression
3. **Premature Task IV** — Don't push "meaning" before Tasks I-III
4. **Grief comparison** — "At least it wasn't sudden." All grief is valid.
5. **Platitudes** — "They're in a better place." Dismisses pain.
6. **Fix-it mode** — Grief must be witnessed, not fixed
7. **Pathologizing normal grief** — Intense pain is normal
8. **Ignoring disenfranchised grief** — Ex-partner, pet, pregnancy, estranged parent
9. **Forcing emotional expression** — Some process through action, not tears
10. **Neglecting helper self-care** — Grief work is heavy
